WEEK 10: Digital Inequalities

In their 2015 article, Robinson et al. delve into the intricate realm of digital inequalities, shedding light on why these discrepancies matter in our increasingly digitalized world. The authors explore a vivid image of the digital divide, illustrating how disparities in access, skills, and usage of digital technologies persist across various socio-economic backgrounds. This divide is not merely about owning the latest gadgets; rather, it encompasses a multifaceted spectrum of disparities that influence one's engagement with the digital realm.

One key insight highlighted by Robinson et al. is the intertwined nature of digital inequalities with broader societal issues, such as education, employment, and social inclusion. We need to recognize the profound impact of digital proficiency on academic success and future career prospects. In places like South Africa, or Africa as a whole, there are massive disparities in technology between the rich and poor, affecting digital profiency on academic success and future career prospects. Those lacking access to digital resources or proficient digital skills face significant barriers in accessing educational materials, job opportunities, and social networks, perpetuating cycles of disadvantage.

In class this week, one of the topics we had on this was how it is difficult for women to enter the tech industry today. During this class, I brought up a point I got from a related reading that explains the reason for this difficulty. The reading covers how race, gender and disability have an effect on how you are treated in the tech industry. How if one does not fit into the "white, hetero-sexual image" that is commonly seen in this industry then they won't get the same recognition or respect as their white male counterparts. This reading was blunt in how it addresses this issue, highlighting factors that influence these inequlities.

Furthermore, Robinson et al. shed light on the ramifications of digital inequalities for democracy and civic engagement. In an era where social media platforms serve as battlegrounds for shaping public discourse, the unequal distribution of digital resources amplifies voices of privilege while silencing marginalized communities. This not only undermines the principles of democratic participation but also exacerbates existing power differentials within society.
