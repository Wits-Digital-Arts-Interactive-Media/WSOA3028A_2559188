Digital Colonialism: What Does that mean for SA software developers?

The 2019 article that covered Sareeta Amrute’s keynote speech titled "Tech Colonialism Today” offers detailed and enlightening practices in the tech industry and how they replicate colonial relationships and behaviours. Such behaviours and relationships include hierarchical structures, exploitative labour, and extractive practices, leading to uneven consequences and malevolent paternalism. These observations highlighted by Amrute provide us with a clear framework for examining the recent retrenchment of Google workers, and the broader implications of that in a South African context regarding our local developers. In this essay I will be exploring these dynamics while relating to both the global and local software development industry and how we can aim to decolonize technology.

In her keynote speech, Amrute speaks on colonial practices that are currently present within the tech industry. By her definition, colonialism is “a historical relationship by which Europe expanded its borders of control, fought wars by proxy, drained the wealth of other territories, established an often-violent system of rule to govern those territories’ populations, instituted a global pattern of slavery and indentured servitude, and developed a rule of law that applied fully only to Europeans themselves, but was taken up by the rest of the world as a mandate for full human rights” (Amrute, 2019 pg. 2). Based on this definition, she goes further by describing colonial relationships as hierarchical, extractive, and exploitative, which lead to uneven consequences and paternalism on those who are colonized.

Based on this, we can first acknowledge that this has been the case for most countries within the Global South for centuries, especially South Africa. Secondly, we can identify that certain colonial practices are being perpetuated within the tech industry today through Amrute's relationship. Google’s decision to retrench hundreds of their workers in May this year while shifting those empty positions to India and Mexico may sound good on paper, but this only serves to reflect several aspects of Amrute’s points on digital colonialism.

The first of these aspects is the hierarchical structures, where power and decision-making are centralized within the Global North, marginalizing those in the Global South. Google’s decision to move their jobs to India and Mexico not only reflects an exploitative approach in employing labour but also perpetuates a form of economic dependency and subordination. A previous reading I had done titled “Decolonizing the Machine: Race, Gender, and Disability in Robots and Algorithmic Art: Abramovic et al.” speaks on the reproduction of existing power dynamics within the art space and how it has filtered into the tech industry and as a result has marginalized certain groups based on race, gender, and disabilities.

What stood out for me the most about this reading was how it addressed the very thing that was happening in Google, “…the field of computer science and popular ideas about technology and the future have hitherto centred around a small and largely homogenous group of people” (Broussard, 2018). Amrute mentions a similar statement by the same data journalist Meredith Broussard who further describes this as techno-chauvinism. Broussard defines techno-chauvinism as “the belief of a small group of fairly homogenous people located primarily in Silicon Valley that are the best people to deploy a small set of algorithmic applications to administer human life” (Broussard, 2018). Based on these two statements, what this tells us is that a “white male, privileged and ignorant” group of board members is making decisions without consulting those it aims to “help” and is blatantly being ignorant by assuming jobs will help those in Mexico or India, thereby exploiting those who are vulnerable enough to take those positions and perpetuating the colonial cycle in which Amrute speaks about.

In addition, the extraction of labour from these regions further proves the perpetuation of these colonial practices, in that workers from India and Mexico are being exploited as cheap labour without adequate pay. These employees are not only seen as a means of cutting costs to Google but also serve as a means of collecting so-called, “raw data” from an “untapped market.” This relationship benefits no one in these Global South regions as it is an unfair, unbalanced exchange of materials, like the relationship between former colonies and their colonizers. Not only that but nowhere in this CNBC article does it mention how the native people of these regions will benefit from this employment, it only serves to paint Google's initial retrenchment in a better light by creating the impression that it will help. Another reading I came across in my research titled “Algorithmic Colonization of Africa” by Abeba Birhane contains a chapter that highlights how big tech companies use tactics like the one Google is doing.

In her chapter titled “Data are People,” Birhane states that “This muting of the individual, a person with fears, emotions, dreams, and hopes, is symptomatic of how little attention is given to matters such as people’s well-being and consent, which should be the primary concerns if the goal is indeed to ‘help’ those in need,” (Birhane, 2020). There was no consultation done by CNBC or Google that genuinely asked the people of Bangalore or Mexico City how they felt about the increase in job opportunities and how it benefits them. But one thing is clear, it is very evident that it is also a way for Google to mine people’s data.

All of this ultimately leads to what Amrute describes as uneven consequences and malevolent paternalism where “Technological innovation’s collateral damage means that the consequences of development are felt unevenly, the harms accruing to black and brown communities in the United States, and Indigenous, poor, and oppressed communities in the Latin American, South Asia, and Africa,” (Amrute, 2019). As much as Google’s restructuring is aimed at improving efficiency, there are still consequences that are unevenly distributed amongst those affected. In the case of this article, the employees from the original core team face job losses while their replacements in the Global South are employed under less favourable conditions. These dynamics highlight the existing inequalities between the regions. Furthermore, this narrative that Google has created jobs in developing countries only masks the exploitative nature of the company which I mentioned earlier. This paternalistic approach is seen as an act of benevolence, but it is a way of exploiting low-cost labour to maximize profits.

Google is not the only company that can be accused of such actions, in fact, compared to trading goods corporations such as Puma and Walmart in a 2013 report it was estimated that 250 million child labourers between the ages of five and fourteen sew clothes for these respective companies and more. It is merely one of many that hide behind the good deed’s façade.

But how does all this affect the South African software development industry you may ask? Well like many other global industries, our technology industry is very much intertwined with that of the rest of the world, especially with tech giants such as Google, Amazon, and Meta. Like many of our other Global South counterparts, it is difficult for developers to enter a space that is so rooted in colonial tendencies and behaviours. Due to this increasingly of our local developers end up becoming outsourced labour, like what is happening in Google. For those who end up leaving the country, finding a job amid Google’s retrenchment and job relocations becomes difficult now that the competition has increased on a global scale. Even if one does land the job, chances are they will be exploited with lower wages and less job security compared to their Global North counterparts.

Though the rate of worker retrenchments in South Africa is not so bad when compared to Google and other tech giants it is still a cause for concern. In a 2024 South African Report titled “State of the Software Developer Nation” by OFFERZEN, 5% of South African developers were retrenched in the past 12 months. Based on the report, even though most of these developers were able to find jobs within the next 3 months of their retrenchment, due to the fact they were senior developers, the junior developers experienced the heavier brunt of this with over 40% of them taking over 6 months to find a new job. Then it comes as a surprise when said juniors accept jobs from outside the country where they end up being exploited out of desperation for a better salary.

With all this information, what can I as an aspiring web developer do in improving this industry and decolonizing it? The first thing is advocating for fair practice in the local companies. Amid frequent junior developer retrenchments, one thing myself and other young developers should do to become engaged in talks on equitable wages, better working conditions, and job security. That way, companies do not find it easy to exploit us. In addition to that, even within the workspace encouraging a team environment allows for bigger issues to be addressed with a larger voice.

Another way of playing a role in the decolonization effort is by promoting and creating local innovations. Supporting tech startups and initiatives that address the needs of the marginalized majority of South Africa will help us build a more resilient and independent tech industry. That way it fosters a sense of innovation and self-reliance where developers reduce reliance on global tech giants. In the same yearly report, it was shown that over half of South Africa’s local developers are working with an AI API. In addition to that, the majority are already working with code-writing assistants. The recent use of AI amongst developers as a working tool is a quick and efficient way to improve the quality of our local developers. Now I know this solution comes with a whole lot of negatives, but with proper administration and monitoring it could aim to help people.

And lastly, learning ethical data use and labour practices is crucial. As mentioned before, big tech companies already have shown us that their tactics for tapping into unknown data regions are invasive to those at a technological disadvantage. One way this can be achieved is by teaching young developers like me design practices that promote transparency and accountability while also being in line with legislation laws that protect its users.

The retrenchment of both local and global developers and the recent relocation of jobs to lower-cost regions highlights the deeply rooted colonial practices within the technology sector as outlined by Amrute. For us in South Africa and as developers, it is important to understand these dynamics so that we can plan for a future that promotes fair practice, and local innovation while ensuring ethical standards that work independently from global tech giants. By doing so, local developers can contribute to a more equitable industry.
